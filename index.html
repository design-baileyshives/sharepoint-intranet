Multifamily Industry Glossary

Key terms used across multifamily operations, leasing, asset management, and compliance.

Categories
Affordable Housing Programs
The midpoint household income for a metropolitan area, published annually by HUD. Used to set income limits on affordable housing programs (e.g., 60% AMI).
The years during which a LIHTC property must meet income and rent restrictions — typically 15 years of strict compliance followed by an extended-use period of at least 15 additional years.
The agreement between HUD (or a public housing authority) and the property owner under which the government pays the difference between the tenant's contribution and the contract rent for Section 8 units.
HUD block-grant program providing funds to states and localities to develop and rehabilitate affordable rental housing, often layered with LIHTC.
The federal agency that administers public housing, Section 8, FHA multifamily mortgage insurance, and fair housing enforcement.
The annual or move-in process of verifying that a household meets the income limits required to occupy a restricted unit. Tenant Income Certifications (TICs) are the standard documentation.
Federal program providing tax credits to developers of affordable rental housing in exchange for income and rent restrictions, typically for a 15- to 30-year compliance period.
Florida statute signed in 2023 and amended in 2024 that preempts local zoning to allow multifamily or mixed-use residential development by-right on commercial, industrial, or mixed-use sites when at least 40% of units are set aside as affordable to households at up to 120% AMI for 30 years.
The recorded agreement that imposes income and rent restrictions on a LIHTC property and runs with the land for the duration of the extended-use period.
Rental assistance tied to specific units in a property under a HAP contract, as opposed to vouchers that move with the tenant.
Tenant-based federal rental assistance (Housing Choice Voucher) under which the housing authority pays a portion of rent directly to a participating landlord. Landlord participation rules vary by state and locality.
Municipal bonds used to finance affordable housing; properties financed with at least 50% tax-exempt bonds qualify for 4% LIHTC credits without competitive allocation.
Construction & Development
The pace at which available units are leased, typically expressed as units per month. The primary measure of lease-up velocity against pro forma.
Municipal authorization that a building is safe and code-compliant for occupancy. Required before residents can move in; often issued building-by-building in phased developments.
A construction contract in which the general contractor commits to deliver the project at or below a fixed ceiling, with savings typically shared or returned to the owner.
Direct construction costs — site work, materials, labor, contractor fees. Distinct from soft costs.
The period from a new property's first delivery of units through achieving stabilized occupancy. Performance is tracked against an absorption schedule established during underwriting.
The projected operating statement used to underwrite a development or acquisition. Establishes the rent, expense, and timing assumptions against which actual performance is measured.
A pre-delivery inspection in which the owner, GC, and architect walk a unit or building to generate the final punch list before turnover.
Non-construction development expenses — architecture, engineering, permits, legal, financing, marketing, lease-up reserves.
The point at which a property reaches sustained occupancy (commonly 90–93%) and operations normalize. Triggers lender milestones and is the standard cutoff for switching from development to operating financials.
An interim CO issued when most but not all final inspections are complete, allowing occupancy while remaining items (often site work or amenities) are finished.
Financial & Operations
Non-recurring spending on improvements that extend a property's useful life — roof replacements, unit renovations, amenity upgrades. Distinct from routine repairs and maintenance.
The ratio of a property's net operating income to its purchase price or current market value, expressed as a percentage. A primary metric used to compare returns across investment properties.
NOI divided by annual debt service. Lenders typically require a minimum DSCR (often 1.20x–1.25x) to underwrite a multifamily loan.
Actual rent collected as a percentage of gross potential rent. Differs from physical occupancy by accounting for concessions, bad debt, and loss-to-lease.
The total rental income a property would generate if every unit were occupied at market rent. The baseline against which vacancy and concessions are measured.
The difference between market rent and the actual rent on in-place leases. A key indicator of pricing opportunity at renewal.
Gross operating income less operating expenses, before debt service, capital expenditures, and income taxes. The core measure of a property's operating performance.
Operating expenses as a percentage of effective gross income. Used to benchmark efficiency across comparable properties.
The percentage of units physically occupied by a resident, regardless of whether rent is being collected.
A rolling twelve-month financial statement used to evaluate recent operating performance during acquisition or refinance underwriting.
Insurance & Risk
An insurance valuation method that pays replacement cost minus depreciation. Generally less favorable for owners than replacement cost coverage.
Coverage for lost rental income while a property is being repaired after a covered loss. Typically expressed as a number of months of coverage.
Documentation evidencing that a vendor, contractor, or resident carries required insurance coverage. Standard prerequisite for vendor onboarding and renter's insurance compliance programs.
The portion of a covered loss the property pays before insurance responds. Often stated as a per-occurrence dollar amount or a percentage of insured value for catastrophe perils.
Coverage for third-party bodily injury and property damage occurring on the property. The foundational liability policy for any multifamily asset.
A separate, typically percentage-based deductible that applies to losses from named hurricanes or windstorms. Material in coastal markets.
First-party coverage for physical damage to buildings and contents from covered perils. Carried by the owner and reflected as an operating expense line.
An owner-administered program (often vendor-supported) requiring residents to carry liability and/or contents coverage and tracking compliance through the lease term.
An insurance valuation method that pays to rebuild without depreciation deduction. Preferred coverage standard for multifamily owners.
Liability coverage that sits above primary GL and auto policies, providing additional limits across underlying coverages. Commonly required by lenders and JV partners.
Investor & Capital Structure
A one-time fee paid to the sponsor at closing, typically 0.5%–2% of the purchase price, compensating for sourcing and underwriting work.
A recurring fee paid to the sponsor — usually a percentage of effective gross revenue or invested equity — for ongoing oversight of the investment.
Annual pre-tax cash flow divided by total equity invested. The most common LP-level return metric for stabilized cash flow.
Total dollars distributed divided by total equity invested over the hold period. Measures absolute return independent of timing.
The sponsor (GP) makes operating decisions and is liable for the partnership; passive investors (LPs) contribute most of the equity and have limited liability.
The discount rate at which the net present value of all cash flows equals zero. The standard time-weighted return measure used to evaluate multifamily investments.
A partnership between a sponsor and an institutional equity partner, typically with a promote structure that rewards the sponsor for exceeding agreed return hurdles.
Subordinate financing that sits between the senior loan and equity, secured by a pledge of ownership interests rather than the real estate. Higher cost than senior debt; preserves equity dollars.
Equity capital that receives a fixed return ahead of common equity but lacks operating control. Often used as a gap-filler alongside senior debt.
The annual return LPs receive before the GP shares in profits — typically 7%–9% in multifamily. May be cumulative or non-cumulative.
The sponsor's disproportionate share of profits earned after LPs receive their preferred return. Often structured in tiers (waterfall) tied to IRR or equity-multiple hurdles.
The contractual order in which distributions flow among partners — return of capital, preferred return, then promote tiers. Defined in the operating agreement.
Leasing & Marketing
Leases signed divided by qualified traffic. Measures leasing-team effectiveness independent of marketing volume.
Any rent reduction or incentive offered to attract or retain a resident — free weeks, waived fees, gift cards. Tracked separately from base rent.
The average number of days a unit remains vacant before being leased. A leading indicator of pricing or product issues.
Stated rent less the amortized value of concessions over the lease term. The true revenue figure used in market comparisons.
Units currently vacant plus units on notice to vacate. The forward-looking view of inventory available to lease.
The rent a unit would command if leased today under current market conditions, independent of in-place lease terms.
Written notice from a resident of intent to move out at lease end or with proper notice. Triggers turn scheduling and re-marketing.
The percentage of expiring leases that renew within a given period. A core retention KPI; benchmarks typically run 50–60%.
Algorithmic or rules-based pricing that adjusts rents daily based on demand, exposure, and competitive set. Common platforms include LRO, YieldStar, and AIRM.
Prospective residents who tour, inquire, or apply. Lead-source attribution of traffic drives marketing spend decisions.
Legal & Compliance
Federal accessibility law applicable to public and common areas of multifamily properties. Distinct from the FHA design and construction requirements that apply to units.
The legal process by which a landlord regains possession of a unit, typically for non-payment of rent or material lease violation. Governed by state-specific procedure and timelines.
Federal law prohibiting housing discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, or disability. State and local laws often add protected classes.
A document attached to and incorporated into the lease that adds, modifies, or clarifies specific terms — pet policy, parking, smoking, lead-based paint disclosure.
A change in rules, policies, or services to allow a resident with a disability equal opportunity to use and enjoy the dwelling. Must be granted unless it imposes an undue burden.
Funds held by the landlord to cover damages beyond normal wear and tear or unpaid rent. State law dictates handling, accounting, and return timelines.
Physical Asset & Maintenance
Property areas shared by residents — leasing office, fitness center, pool, hallways, parking. Maintenance costs are reflected in operating expenses, not unit pricing.
Repairs and replacements that have been postponed beyond their normal cycle. A standard diligence item; quantified in inspection reports.
A unit that has completed the turn process and is available to lease and move in. Tracked daily against exposure.
Scheduled service on building systems — HVAC, boilers, pool equipment — performed to extend useful life and avoid failure. Tracked by work-order systems.
The itemized list of remaining repairs or finishes on a new construction or renovated unit prior to delivery.
Individual measurement of utility usage at the unit level (water, electric, gas), enabling resident billback rather than bundling into rent.
The process of preparing a vacated unit for the next resident — cleaning, painting, repairs, inspection. Turn time directly impacts vacancy loss.
An investment strategy involving targeted capital improvements (in-unit renovations, amenity upgrades, exterior refresh) to drive rent growth and asset value.
Technology Stack
Conversational tools (chat, email, SMS, voice) that respond to prospect inquiries, qualify leads, and schedule tours 24/7. Examples include Lisa, Betty, Quext, and Meet Elise.
Reporting and analytics platforms that consolidate PMS, CRM, and operational data into dashboards — portfolio occupancy, leasing velocity, expense variance. Examples include RealPage IMS, Yardi Elevate, and custom Power BI stacks.
Software that tracks prospects through the leasing funnel — inquiries, tours, applications, follow-ups. Often integrated with the PMS; examples include Knock, Funnel, and Yardi RentCafe CRM.
Third-party rental marketplaces where vacant units are advertised — Apartments.com, Zillow, Rent.com, ApartmentList. A primary traffic source and major marketing-budget line.
The system of record for unit inventory, leases, rent roll, accounting, and resident data. Common platforms include Yardi Voyager, RealPage OneSite, Entrata, ResMan, and AppFolio.
A prospect-led tour using temporary access credentials issued after identity verification. Extends touring hours and reduces leasing-team load; requires smart-lock and ID-verification infrastructure.
Connected in-unit devices — smart locks, thermostats, leak sensors, lighting — managed centrally. Enables self-guided tours, contactless turn, and operational efficiency.
Utility Billing & Ancillary Income
Non-rent revenue streams — pet rent, parking, storage, application fees, late fees, valet trash, utility reimbursements. A key lever for total income growth without raising base rent.
A recurring monthly fee charged in addition to a one-time pet deposit or non-refundable pet fee. Distinct from service or emotional support animals, which cannot be charged under fair housing rules.
A method of allocating master-metered utility costs to residents based on a formula — typically occupants, square footage, or a blend — when submetering is not feasible. Subject to state-specific disclosure and calculation rules.
Resident-paid charges that offset the property's utility expense, recovered via submetering or RUBS. Typically shown net against utility expense in financial reporting.
A doorstep waste-collection service in which residents leave trash outside their unit on scheduled nights for pickup. Billed as a monthly fee separate from rent; a common ancillary income line.
